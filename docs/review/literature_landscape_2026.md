# Literature Landscape & Competitive Analysis (March 2026)

## 1. Screen Time Debate: Current State (2019-2026)

The screen time debate has been running for ~7 years with no convergence. Two camps remain entrenched.

### Regulatory Camp ("Screen time causes harm")
| Reference | Key Claim |
|-----------|-----------|
| Twenge, J. M. (2017). *iGen*. Atria Books. | Youth mental illness rise parallels smartphone adoption |
| Haidt, J. (2024). *The Anxious Generation*. Penguin Press. | "Great Rewiring" of childhood via smartphones/social media |
| Hansen, A. (2019). *Skärmhjärnan*. Bonnier Fakta. | Global bestseller (JP: スマホ脳). Brain not evolved for digital stimuli |
| Boers, E. et al. (2019). JAMA Pediatrics, 173(9), 853-859. | Screen time associated with depression in adolescence |

### Digital Resilience Camp ("Effects are trivially small")
| Reference | Key Claim |
|-----------|-----------|
| Orben, A. & Przybylski, A. K. (2019). Nature Human Behaviour, 3(2), 173-182. | Effect sizes comparable to "wearing glasses or eating potatoes" |
| Coyne, S. M. et al. (2020). Computers in Human Behavior, 104, 106160. | 8-year longitudinal: no lasting mental health harm |
| Allcott, H. et al. (2020). American Economic Review, 110(3), 629-676. | Facebook deactivation RCT: modest well-being gains BUT reduced political knowledge |
| Johannes, N. et al. (2021). Royal Society Open Science, 8(2), 202049. | Video game play positively correlated with well-being |

### Why this matters for our paper
Our Introduction (Section 1) criticizes both camps: "both camps are looking at the wrong variable." This claim MUST be grounded in proper citations of both sides. As of the March 2026 expansion, we cite Hansen, Orben & Przybylski, Coyne et al., and Allcott et al. in addition to the original Haidt and Twenge.

## 2. Latest Research (2025-2026)

### Key 2025-2026 findings that SUPPORT our framework

| Reference | Finding | Relevance to Cognitive Obesity |
|-----------|---------|-------------------------------|
| Nature HSS Comm (2026). "Excessive screen time is associated with mental health problems: physical activity and sleep as parallel mediators" | Screen time harms mental health *through displacement of physical activity and sleep* | **Direct support for balance model** — harm comes from reducing Term 2 (processing), not from Term 1 (input) alone |
| Li et al. (2025). British J Clinical Psych. | Bidirectional associations; longitudinal effects weaker than cross-sectional | Consistent with balance model: screen time alone is weak predictor; balance matters |
| CDC (2025). Screen Time and Health Outcomes Among US Teenagers. | Each additional hour → only 0.1-0.3 point PHQ-A increase | Small effect sizes when measuring only input side |
| BMC Public Health (2024). ABCD Study. | Bidirectional effects: symptoms → screen time as much as screen time → symptoms | Supports our claim that screen time is the wrong variable |

### Closest competitor paper

**Frontiers in Digital Health (2025): "Redefining psychopathology in the context of digital overload: emerging disorders in the age of information saturation"**
- URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/digital-health/articles/10.3389/fdgth.2025.1693287/full
- Proposes 4 new diagnostic categories for digital-era psychopathology
- Evidence: 75 clinicians surveyed + 225 patient records
- **Critical differences from our framework:**
  - NO balance model (input-side only)
  - NO sensorimotor loop closure concept
  - NO international panel data (177 countries)
  - NO direct quantitative test of additive vs. ratio model
  - Descriptive classification, not predictive/falsifiable framework

**Frontiers in Psychology (2025): "Cognitive offloading or cognitive overload? How AI alters the mental architecture of coping"**
- URL: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1699320/full
- Examines AI's paradoxical effect: reduces immediate cognitive burden but creates long-term overload
- Relevant to our Section 7 (AI implications) but does not propose balance model

## 3. Theoretical Precursors (added March 2026)

These were added to Section 2 of both EN/JA papers to properly position the loop closure framework.

| Theory | Reference | Connection to Loop Closure |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Flow | Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1990). *Flow*. Harper & Row. | Flow = tight sensorimotor loop closure; self-referential cognition ceases |
| Rumination | Nolen-Hoeksema, S. (1991). J Abnorm Psychol, 100(4), 569-582. | Rumination = open cognitive loops with no behavioral closure (pathological) |
| Self-Determination Theory | Ryan, R. M. & Deci, E. L. (2000). American Psychologist, 55(1), 68-78. | Competence & autonomy require closed action-outcome loops |
| Embodied Cognition | Varela, F. J., Thompson, E. & Rosch, E. (1991). *The Embodied Mind*. MIT Press. | Cognition = sensorimotor coupling, not disembodied information processing |
| Attention Restoration | Kaplan, S. (1995). J Environmental Psychology, 15(3), 169-182. | Nature restores attention by engaging bottom-up (sensorimotor) while resting top-down (cognitive) |

**Key insight**: Each of these theories independently identifies the same distinction (closed sensorimotor loop = adaptive; open cognitive loop = pathological) but none unifies them under a single quantitative model. That's our contribution.

## 4. Novelty Assessment

### What IS novel in our paper:
1. **Structural explanation for why the screen time debate doesn't converge** — both camps measure only input (Term 1); balance requires Term 2
2. **Sensorimotor loop closure as unifying concept** — Flow, rumination, SDT, embodied cognition, ART all re-described via loop closure degree
3. **Additive balance model with direct empirical test** — L = α₁I − α₂C beats R = I/C (ΔAIC = 86, N = 21,736)
4. **Quantified nutritional analogy** — Whitworth (2009) proposed metaphor; we provide 177-country panel evidence
5. **Interaction non-significance as theoretical prediction** — p = 0.95 confirms independent additive channels

### What is NOT novel (well-established prior findings):
- Physical activity reduces depression (Singh 2023, Noetel 2024: massive meta-analyses)
- Passive vs. active media use distinction (Verduyn 2015)
- Information overload harms mental health (Whitworth 2009, Frontiers 2025)
- Screen time effect sizes are small (Orben & Przybylski 2019)

### Key positioning statement:
> Individual elements are known. The novelty is (a) formalizing the processing side (Term 2) as an independent additive channel, (b) unifying five theoretical traditions under loop closure, and (c) providing cross-national quantitative evidence for the balance structure.
